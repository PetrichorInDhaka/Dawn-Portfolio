Project Overview

This analysis evaluates the profitability and competitive positioning of five major U.S. airline routes using simulated BTS DB1B and T-100 style market data. The objective was to assess pricing behavior, operational efficiency, and revenue performance across competing carriers and identify opportunities for strategic route expansion.

Routes analyzed: JFK–LAX, DFW–ORD, ATL–MIA, SFO–SEA, BOS–DCA, and proposed NYC–AUS.

Metrics evaluated: Average Fare, Load Factor, RASM, Yield, Market Share, HHI, and Flight Frequency.

Key Findings

  • Premium routes outperform. JFK–LAX and BOS–DCA led in revenue due to higher fares and stronger business demand. Legacy carriers held pricing power despite LCC competition.
  • Seasonal fare spikes confirmed. Fares peaked clearly in summer (Jun–Aug) and the December holiday period, with strong load factors sustaining revenue during peaks.
  • Load factor drives yield. Routes above 75% load factor consistently outperformed in profitability metrics — seat utilization directly correlates with revenue efficiency.
  • Concentration protects margins. Higher HHI markets showed elevated average fares and reduced pricing pressure, enabling stronger carrier profitability.
Recommendation
Launch NYC–Austin Nonstop Service

The NYC–Austin route demonstrates strong forecast demand growth driven by Austin's technology sector expansion and corporate relocation activity. Projected load factors exceed break-even thresholds, fare levels remain sustainable, and the competitive environment allows room for additional capacity. Business traveler mix supports premium pricing with scalable frequency growth.

Methodology — Route Profitability

01
Data Extraction. Aggregated BTS DB1B data to filter and isolate the five target routes for analysis.
02
Metric Development. Calculated Average Fare, Load Factor, Yield, RASM, Market Share, and Profit Margin per route and carrier.
03
Trend Analysis. Examined monthly fare movements to identify seasonal pricing patterns and competitive pricing pressure periods.
04
Performance Comparison. Used Load Factor vs. Yield to distinguish carriers buying market share with low fares from those extracting a premium.

RASM was chosen over total revenue to enable fair comparison across routes of different distances and aircraft sizes, ensuring short-haul and long-haul routes can be evaluated on a consistent basis.

Methodology — Demand Forecasting

01
Demand Analysis. Analyzed BTS T-100 data to understand existing connecting passenger traffic on the NYC–Austin route.
02
Competitive Review. Reviewed carrier frequency, pricing patterns, and concentration levels across existing market participants.
03
Schedule Modelling. Modelled a proposed schedule of three daily flights to capture both business and day-trip demand segments.
04
Break-even Assessment. Compared forecast demand against break-even load factor thresholds to assess commercial sustainability.

Combining actual market demand, competitive conditions, and growth trends produces a realistic view of route potential before launch decisions are made.
